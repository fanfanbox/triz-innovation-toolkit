# Chapter 19: TRIZ for Business & Management

## Core Idea
TRIZ for Business adapts the classical TRIZ contradiction-resolution approach to business, management, and organizational problems. Instead of technical parameters like "weight vs strength," it works with business parameters like "profit vs time-to-market" and resolves them through 12 double principles (inventive principles paired with their antithesis for business contexts), adapted from Livotov's TRIZ Handbook.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **12 Double Business TRIZ Principles**: Each principle is presented as a pair — the principle AND its opposite — because business problems often require balancing contradictory strategies rather than choosing one.
  - When to use: Business model innovation, organizational restructuring, strategy conflicts, process improvement, marketing contradictions.
  - When NOT to use: Purely technical engineering problems (use classical TRIZ). Simple financial calculations. Legal/regulatory issues where creativity is constrained.

- **5 TRIZ Thinking Components for Business**: A simplified mental framework for applying TRIZ thinking to business contexts:
  1. Ideality (maximize value, minimize cost/harm)
  2. Contradictions (identify and resolve business trade-offs)
  3. Resources (find untapped business resources)
  4. Patterns (recognize business evolution trends)
  5. Effects (find business mechanisms that deliver desired outcomes)

- **Business Problem Mapping**: Translating business problems into TRIZ-compatible statements.
  - When to use: When a business problem feels too vague or qualitative for TRIZ tools.

## Key Concepts

- **Business Contradiction**: Same structure as technical contradiction but with business parameters. "Improving customer satisfaction (A) increases operational cost (B)."
- **Double Principle**: In business, every inventive principle has a valid opposite. Principle 1 (Segmentation) vs Anti-Principle 1 (Consolidation). The skill is knowing when to apply which — or how to apply BOTH simultaneously.
- **Business Ideality**: Σ Customer Value / (Σ Costs + Σ Harms). The same equation, applied to business models instead of technical systems.
- **Business Resources**: Underutilized assets — brand equity, customer data, employee knowledge, partner networks, idle capacity, regulatory advantages — that can be mobilized at near-zero marginal cost.
- **Business Trimming**: Eliminating organizational components (departments, processes, intermediaries) while maintaining or improving the function they served.
- **Market S-Curve**: Products, business models, and even entire industries follow the same S-curve evolution as technologies — infancy, growth, maturity, decline.

## The 12 Double Business TRIZ Principles

| # | Principle | Anti-Principle | Application |
|---|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Segmentation** — break into smaller units | **Consolidation** — merge for scale | Spin-offs vs mergers; microservices vs monolith |
| 2 | **Taking Out** — separate core from non-core | **Integration** — bundle everything | Outsourcing vs vertical integration |
| 3 | **Local Quality** — tailor to context | **Standardization** — uniform everywhere | Customization vs global standardization |
| 4 | **Asymmetry** — break symmetry for advantage | **Symmetry** — balanced design | Asymmetric business models vs balanced portfolios |
| 5 | **Merging** — combine for synergy | **Separation** — keep independent | Partnerships vs standalone competition |
| 6 | **Universality** — one thing does many things | **Specialization** — focus on one thing deeply | Platform plays vs niche focus |
| 7 | **Nested Doll** — layers within layers | **Flat Structure** — remove hierarchy | Multi-tier distribution vs DTC (direct-to-consumer) |
| 8 | **Anti-Weight** — compensate weakness with external help | **Self-Sufficiency** — build in-house | Strategic alliances vs insourcing |
| 9 | **Preliminary Anti-Action** — preempt problems | **Reactive Action** — respond to events | Risk management vs agile response |
| 10 | **Preliminary Action** — prepare in advance | **Just-in-Time** — act at the last moment | Forward planning vs lean/agile |
| 11 | **Cushion in Advance** — build buffers | **Zero Slack** — eliminate all waste | Inventory buffers vs just-in-time delivery |
| 12 | **Equipotentiality** — equal access for all | **Hierarchy** — tiered access/privileges | Flat org vs traditional hierarchy |

## Mental Models

- **Every business trade-off is a contradiction to resolve, not a compromise to accept.** The core TRIZ insight applied to business: "We can either have high growth OR high profitability" is a contradiction to be resolved, not a constraint to optimize within.
- **Business problems are under-defined compared to engineering problems.** In engineering, parameters are measurable. In business, "customer satisfaction" and "brand value" are fuzzy. The skill is translating fuzzy business parameters into specific, measurable proxies.
- **Business resources are invisible to their owners.** Just as engineers overlook "free" resources (gravity, ambient heat), business leaders overlook their most powerful assets (customer trust, employee creativity, data exhaust) because they're not on the balance sheet.
- **The opposite of a good business idea is often another good business idea.** The 12 double principles encode this — consolidation is as valid as segmentation. The art is choosing the right principle for the specific context, not declaring one universal truth.

## Worked Example: A Restaurant Chain's Growth Dilemma

**Problem**: A restaurant chain wants to grow but finds that each new location reduces overall quality consistency.

**Business Contradiction**:
- Improving: Number of locations (growth)
- Worsening: Food quality consistency

**Apply Double Principles**:

| Principle | How Applied |
|-----------|------------|
| P1 (Segmentation) | Franchise instead of company-owned — local owners maintain quality incentives |
| P2 (Taking Out) | Centralize food prep (commissary kitchen) — separate cooking from assembly |
| P3 (Local Quality) | Core menu standardized; 20% menu localized to regional tastes |
| P10 (Preliminary Action) | Pre-train managers at existing locations before new store opens |
| Anti-P4 (Symmetry) | Balanced scorecard: growth AND quality metrics equally weighted in bonuses |

**Resolution**: Hybrid model — company-owned in core cities (quality control), franchised in secondary markets (growth), centralized prep kitchen (Taking Out) eliminates the contradiction between growth and consistency.

## Anti-patterns

- **Applying engineering TRIZ directly to business problems**: The 39 engineering parameters (weight, stress, temperature) don't map to business. Always translate to business parameters first.
- **Treating the 12 double principles as a menu to pick one from**: The power is in combining principles into a coherent strategy. "We'll use Segmentation" is not a strategy; "We'll segment the customer base (P1) while consolidating back-office operations (Anti-P1)" is.
- **Ignoring the anti-principle**: Every business principle has a valid opposite. Before committing to a principle, deliberately consider whether the anti-principle serves your context better.

## Key Takeaways

1. TRIZ for Business uses the same contradiction-resolution engine as classical TRIZ, but adapted for business parameters and strategies
2. The 12 Double Principles present each inventive principle WITH its valid opposite — business problems often require both
3. Translate fuzzy business parameters into specific, measurable proxies before applying TRIZ tools
4. Business resources (brand, data, relationships, knowledge) are the most overlooked source of low-cost innovation
5. Every "either/or" business trade-off is a contradiction waiting to be resolved, not a constraint to be accepted
6. The 5 thinking components (Ideality, Contradictions, Resources, Patterns, Effects) provide a mental checklist for business TRIZ
