# Chapter 4: Applying the Trends of Technical Evolution

## Core Idea
Technical systems evolve following predictable patterns, just as biological systems do. By mapping your product onto these 8 Trends, you can systematically predict its next generations -- and file strong patents to protect that future before competitors reach it.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 8 Trends of Technical Evolution**: conceptual patterns of how systems develop over time. They are observed (not invented) from analysis of diverse technologies.
  1. **Increasing Ideality**: systems improve over time -- more benefits, fewer costs, fewer harms. The most fundamental Trend.
  2. **S-Curves**: system improvement follows a four-phase lifecycle: Infancy (development, low Ideality) -> Youth (rapid improvement, new benefits added) -> Maturity (slowing improvement, focus on cost reduction) -> Old Age (Ideality plateaus or declines; prepare for replacement).
  3. **Increasing System Coordination** (Non-Uniform Development of Parts): different parts of a system develop at different rates. Identify under-developed components -- improving them yields the largest system-level gains.
  4. **Increasing System Automation** (Less Human Involvement): responsibility moves from human -> mechanical -> powered -> automatic -> self-adjusting -> decision-making. (Technology often exists for the final step; social acceptance is the barrier.)
  5. **Increasing Segmentation and Use of Fields**: systems break into smaller pieces over time, eventually becoming field effects (e.g., soap bar -> flakes -> powder -> liquid -> gel -> ultrasonic cleaning).
  6. **Increasing Dynamism and Controllability**: systems become more flexible and variable (rigid -> hinged -> elastic -> field-based), requiring correspondingly more sophisticated control.
  7. **Simplicity-Complexity-Simplicity**: systems start simple, gain complexity by adding components, then integrate everything into a simpler unified system (e.g., smartphone absorbing camera, alarm clock, pedometer, diary).
  8. **Better Matching of Needs** (Matching and Mismatching): systems become increasingly better tuned to all user needs over time.

- **S-Curve Analysis** (a framework within Trend #2):
  - When to use: to determine WHERE your product is in its lifecycle and what kind of innovation to invest in.
  - How: plot Ideality (not just value/cost) against time. Identify the current phase: Infancy -> solve big problems to reach market. Youth -> add new benefits, resolve contradictions. Maturity -> focus on cost reduction and incremental improvement. Old Age -> invest in the NEXT S-Curve.

- **Next-Generation System Development Process**:
  - When to use: for any existing product or service that needs a roadmap.
  - How: (1) pick a Trend, (2) identify where your current system sits on it, (3) use the next step as a trigger for ideas, (4) repeat for every step up to the endpoint, (5) pick a new Trend and repeat, (6) apply at three levels of scale: super-system (surrounding environment), system (your product as a whole), and sub-system (individual components).

## Key Concepts
- **Trend of Technical Evolution**: a general direction in which systems change -- not a law or rule, but a documented pattern. Not every Trend applies to every system; some steps may be skipped.
- **S-Curve**: the non-linear pattern of system improvement over time, mapping Ideality growth through Infancy, Youth, Maturity, and Old Age.
- **Transition to the Super-System**: the ultimate destiny of any system is to disappear -- either the need vanishes, or the surrounding super-system absorbs the function (e.g., cloud storage replacing local hard drives; vaccination eliminating the need for treatment).
- **Voice of the Product**: the DNA of the product itself tells you how it will evolve. Listen to it alongside the voice of the customer. The Trends reveal this internal logic.
- **Jumping S-Curves**: a new technology typically has LOWER Ideality than the mature technology it replaces at launch, but its steeper S-Curve trajectory means it will overtake. You must invest in new S-Curves before the old one peaks.
- **Sub-Trends**: some Trends decompose into finer lines of evolution (e.g., Increasing Segmentation applies to the object, the surface, the internal space, AND the rhythm of the system).

## Mental Models
- **Use "voice of the product" alongside voice of the customer.** Market research cannot predict disruptive change because the product itself shapes the market. Map your system onto the Trends to see where it is heading regardless of what customers say today.
- **Use S-Curves to decide where to invest.** In Youth, invest in adding benefits and solving big contradictions. In Maturity, invest in cost reduction. In late Maturity, invest in the NEXT S-Curve even though it currently looks worse.
- **Use Simplicity-Complexity-Simplicity when your product has accumulated too many bolt-on features.** Look at all the separate components and functions; the next generation integrates them into a unified system.
- **Use "ultimate destiny" thinking for long-term strategy.** If your system will eventually disappear into the super-system, plan for that transition rather than being blindsided by it. Your business does not need to disappear -- find the new market (Wilkinson Sword moved from swords to razors).

## Anti-patterns
- **Focusing development on the parts you understand best**: Increasing System Coordination says different parts develop at different rates. You are probably over-developing your comfort zone and neglecting the components that are holding the whole system back.
- **Kodak syndrome -- failing to jump S-Curves**: Kodak held an early patent for digital photography but was so invested in film that it failed to pivot. A new S-Curve looks worse at first (lower Ideality) but overtakes. If you are not investing in the next generation, a competitor is.
- **Assuming the Trends are laws every system must follow exactly**: Trends suggest direction, not destiny. Not every Trend applies to every system. Some steps are skipped. Multiple Trends should be explored simultaneously.
- **Ignoring shifts in Ideality boundaries**: when you widen your scope (e.g., include environmental impact), costs and harms that were previously invisible become visible. This can move your system BACKWARD on a Trend -- and that is correct behavior (e.g., cloth nappies returning as environmental awareness grows).

## Worked Example
**The evolution of detergent for cleaning clothes** (Increasing Segmentation and Use of Fields Trend):
1. Bar of soap -- a single solid object
2. Soap flakes -- segmented into small pieces
3. Powders -- finer segmentation
4. Liquids and gels -- further segmented, almost field-like
5. Ultrasonic washing machine -- a FIELD EFFECT replaces the substance entirely; cleaning happens via ultrasound waves

Each step increases the segmentation of the cleaning agent until it becomes a pure field. The washing machine example (recently entering the domestic market) shows the Trend reaching its endpoint: the detergent as a physical substance disappears entirely.

**Digital cameras vs. film cameras** (S-Curve jump): When first launched, digital cameras were worse than film cameras -- more expensive, bigger, heavier, lower image quality. But they offered many new benefits (no film cost, instant review, easy sharing), and as the technology (and supporting infrastructure: computers, internet, storage) improved, digital cameras overtook film cameras and wiped out their market. Kodak, despite holding an early digital photography patent, failed to make the jump.

## Key Takeaways
1. The Trends are your product roadmap -- they chart the likely future trajectory from your product's past through its present.
2. Always assess your product's position on the S-Curve before deciding what kind of innovation to pursue. The phase determines whether to add benefits, cut costs, or jump to the next technology.
3. Listen to the "voice of the product" as seriously as the voice of the customer. The product's own DNA reveals development paths that customers cannot articulate.
4. The ultimate destiny of every system is to disappear -- prepare for the transition to the super-system before it is forced upon you.
5. Improving the most under-developed component of your system (Increasing System Coordination) often yields the biggest overall gain.
6. The Trends are spectacularly powerful for patent strategy: apply all 8 Trends to your system and its components to generate defensive patents covering future embodiments before competitors reach them.
7. Apply the Trends at three scales simultaneously: super-system (what is the environment doing?), system (what is your product doing?), and sub-system (what are the components doing?).
