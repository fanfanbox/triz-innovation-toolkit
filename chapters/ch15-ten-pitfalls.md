# Chapter 15: Ten Pitfalls to Avoid

## Core Idea
Most TRIZ failures come not from the tools being inadequate but from how people approach them -- picking the wrong problem, the wrong scope, or the wrong mindset. Avoiding these ten common mistakes dramatically increases your odds of generating useful, implementable solutions.

## Frameworks Introduced
(No new TRIZ frameworks are introduced in this chapter. The chapter is a meta-level guide to the effective use of all the frameworks covered in the rest of the book.)

## Key Concepts

### The Ten Pitfalls

1. **Thinking TRIZ Doesn't Apply to You**
   Believing your industry's problems are completely unique. The logic of TRIZ is that the same fundamental problems recur across all industries and applications. TRIZ re-applies tried-and-tested solutions from engineering and science, and also models the thinking patterns of creative problem solvers -- both are domain-agnostic. The author has applied TRIZ in biosciences, local government, education, marketing, consumer goods, industrial chemistry, software, management, construction, product design, and packaging. At the heart of TRIZ is the philosophy that your situation is not unique and you can learn from the experience of others.

2. **Waiting for the 'Right' Problem**
   Holding off until a suitably complicated problem appears that "matches" the TRIZ process or seems hard enough to justify its use. TRIZ works on any kind of problem. Start with whatever issue you are facing right now, even if it seems trivial. Do not worry about using TRIZ perfectly -- use the tools, reflect on the process and outcomes, and improve next time. You are better off using a bit of TRIZ a lot of the time than all the TRIZ tools never.

3. **Starting Too Big**
   Attempting to solve the most difficult, intractable problem your industry has grappled with for 30 years as your first TRIZ project. Large, complex problems are daunting when you are still learning the tools. Work your way up: start with smaller, preferably urgent problems. The analogy is running -- you would not start by attempting a marathon on your first day; you build up through shorter races. If you must start with a difficult problem, work with an experienced TRIZ practitioner who can guide you.

4. **Tackling Problems for Which You Can't Implement Solutions**
   Choosing problems outside your scope of influence (world peace, changing your boss's behavior). You may devise brilliant solutions but be unable to put them into practice, making problem solving merely an academic exercise that leaves you discouraged. Reframe the problem to make it implementable by you -- instead of "change my boss," ask "what can I do to deal better with senior management."

5. **Tackling Problems without Involving the Problem Owner**
   Excluding the people responsible for the issue because they were involved in its creation or may resist new thinking. This is exactly why they must be involved: they possess crucial, detailed inside knowledge no one else has. They can identify what may prevent your solutions from working, allowing early resolution of those issues. Critically, problem owners are the people who will implement the solutions -- being involved in development motivates them to implement, while being handed someone else's ideas disenfranchises them.

6. **Trying to Solve Problems When You Don't Understand the Issue or the Technology**
   Attempting TRIZ on a system whose workings you do not grasp. TRIZ is not a substitute for domain knowledge -- it supplements, extends, and makes best use of your knowledge. If you do not understand how individual elements work, get the experts in the room or learn it yourself first. You would not ask a surgeon to build a bridge.

7. **Trying to Solve Problems When You Lack Crucial Knowledge**
   This is subtly different from Pitfall 6: you may understand the technology but lack the specific data needed. To improve safety, you need to know where, why, and when dangers exist. To cut costs, you need to know exactly what each element costs and all the useful things it does. Without this data, you are guessing, not problem solving. A caveat: TRIZ can help you work out precisely what you need to know and give you structured questions to find it efficiently.

8. **Working on a Problem That's Already Been Solved**
   Re-applying TRIZ to a known problem to "see if it arrives at the same solution." This fails for two reasons: (a) the known answer creates psychological inertia -- every TRIZ direction seems to point to it (like being told "don't think of a blue penguin"); (b) people lack motivation because the outcome is already known, removing the challenge that drives creative problem solving.

9. **Undertaking TRIZ by Stealth**
   Trying to cunningly lead colleagues through the TRIZ process without telling them why. They will become suspicious about why you are directing their thinking and likely resist. Instead, be open about the logic of what you are doing and why. Start small: a 30-60 minute session on Ideal Outcome, Time and Scale mapping, or directed brainstorming with the 40 Inventive Principles. You can still shape conversations with TRIZ thinking -- asking about ideal outcomes, searching for analogies, identifying concepts behind ideas -- without explicitly naming it as TRIZ.

10. **Giving Up Too Soon**
    Abandoning the process when solutions are not emerging as expected. Instead: take a break, re-examine your problem scope, check if you skipped steps, verify your Ideal Outcome is truly ideal (not pragmatically watered down). If you have imperfect solutions, develop them further -- construct an Ideality Plot, identify the contradictions in your best-but-flawed ideas, and solve those contradictions. The author has never worked through the TRIZ process and run out of something to do. Biographies of great inventors always describe a point where something went wrong and they kept trying.

## Mental Models
- Use "Reframe to Your Sphere of Influence" when a problem feels overwhelming: if you cannot implement solutions to the problem as stated, the problem is scoped too broadly -- ask what aspect of it you can actually change.
- Use "Involve the Implementer" when forming a problem-solving team: the person who will execute the solution must be in the room during its creation, both for their detailed knowledge and for their buy-in.
- Use "Safe-to-Fail Practice" when starting with TRIZ: your first attempts will be imperfect, so deliberately choose a small, non-critical problem with supportive colleagues so you can learn the process without high stakes.

## Anti-patterns
- **Domain exceptionalism**: "our problems are unique" is itself a failure of TRIZ thinking -- TRIZ exists precisely because problems repeat across domains.
- **Perfection paralysis**: waiting for the perfect problem or perfect conditions means you never start. Use TRIZ on whatever is in front of you today.
- **Solution tourism**: solving problems where you cannot implement the solutions is satisfying in the moment but produces no real-world value and breeds cynicism.
- **Stealth facilitation**: hiding the TRIZ process from participants creates suspicion and resistance; transparency builds trust and engagement.

## Key Takeaways
1. TRIZ applies to any domain because the same fundamental problems recur everywhere -- if you think your field is the exception, you are falling for Pitfall 1.
2. Start small with a real, urgent, implementable problem where you control the outcome -- do not wait for the "right" problem or tackle world peace as your first session.
3. Always involve the problem owner -- they have irreplaceable knowledge and will be the one implementing the solution, so their buy-in is essential.
4. TRIZ is a supplement to domain expertise, not a substitute -- you must understand how your system works before you can improve it.
5. Never work on already-solved problems as a test of TRIZ -- the known answer creates psychological inertia that blocks genuine creative thinking.
6. Be transparent with colleagues about using TRIZ -- a short, focused session on one tool (Ideal Outcome, 40 Principles, Time and Scale) is a better introduction than a stealth process.
7. Keep going when stuck -- re-scope, re-idealize, find the contradictions in your imperfect ideas, and solve those. There is always more TRIZ to apply.
