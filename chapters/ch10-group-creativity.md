# Chapter 10: Problem Solving and Being Creative with Others

## Core Idea
TRIZ provides both the psychological attitude (start by believing you can get everything you want) and the social techniques (Bad Solution Park, hybridisation, structured divergence/convergence) that make collaborative creative problem solving reliable rather than fragile.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Going for What You Really Want (the TRIZ Attitude)**:
  - When to use: at the very start of any problem-solving effort, as a mental posture before applying any tools.
  - How: begin by listing all the things you want and trying to achieve as many as possible. State that improvement is always possible. Do not start with "what is the easiest, smallest change?" -- that can be a constraint you add later, but the initial attitude must be expansive. This approach counters the negative spiral of "this is impossible" and replaces it with confidence in a systematic process.

- **The Bad Solution Park**:
  - When to use: throughout the entire problem-solving process -- at the start to capture initial ideas, and continuously as new ideas arise during analysis stages.
  - How: create a wall space (physical or virtual) where anyone can post solutions at any time. Call them "Bad Solutions" to acknowledge they are imperfect and can be improved. Go around the room asking each person for one solution at a time to ensure equal contribution. Review the Park periodically. This lowers the threshold for sharing -- a "really terrible idea" is easier to offer than a supposedly brilliant one.

- **Ideality Audit for Solution Improvement**:
  - When to use: after generating solutions, to systematically find and fix their imperfections.
  - How: list all benefits the solution delivers (identify insufficient ones for Standard Solutions), all costs (consider trimming to reduce), all harms (apply Standard Solutions for harms). Also drive solutions up the Trends of Technical Evolution. This turns any solution into an iterative improvement engine.

- **Problem-Solving Table (Good/Bad/Contradiction)**:
  - When to use: for quick improvement of a specific existing solution, particularly new or not-yet-well-defined ones.
  - How: create columns: What Do I Want? | Bad Solutions | What's Good? | What's Bad? | TRIZ Solution Trigger | Better Solution. Translate the good and bad into Technical or Physical Contradictions, then look up the relevant Inventive Principles.

- **Hybridisation (Cabbage-Carrot)**:
  - When to use: after multiple solutions have been generated and shared, to synthesize the best elements of each.
  - How: identify the "plus points" of each individual solution and combine them. A cabbage has useful leaves but useless roots; a carrot has useful roots but useless leaves; a hybrid would have both. Run a session explicitly dedicated to combining and hybridizing solutions.

## Key Concepts

- **Psychological Inertia**: a mental rut where unconscious assumptions (often based on previous success) prevent you from seeing a problem as it really is. The nine-dots puzzle demonstrates this -- fewer than 5% of people solve it because they construct a false "box" constraint.
- **Self-System**: a solution where the cause of the problem automatically provides the solution, requiring no external power or human intervention. Example: a road sign that changes color when cold, warning of ice -- the cold (the problem) triggers the warning.
- **Bad Solution**: any initial, top-of-head idea that is imperfect but contains useful information about what you want and what is preventing you from getting it. The term deliberately lowers the threshold for sharing.
- **Ugly Baby**: a beloved solution that its creator thinks is perfect but everyone else sees as flawed. Understanding this dynamic helps teams accept critique without defensiveness.
- **Iterative Process**: TRIZ problem solving is not done once; you repeat the process on solutions you generate, each cycle bringing you closer to the ideal.
- **Ideality Audit**: systematically checking a solution against the Ideality equation -- listing all benefits, costs, and harms -- to identify every opportunity for improvement.
- **Innovative Climate**: the organizational conditions for innovation: resources, support for creative work, risk tolerance, autonomy, information sharing, diversity, clear vision, focus, skill-matching, and training.
- **Constraints -- Real or Imagined?**: many constraints that inhibit creative thinking are self-imposed. Challenging them (especially by working across departments) often reveals that what one group assumes is fixed, another group is willing to change.

## Mental Models

- Use "what would make this solution better?" when looking at any solution (yours or others') -- not "is this solution good or bad?" Every solution has both good and bad aspects; identifying both is the raw material for systematic improvement.
- Use "reframe the blocker as a TRIZ problem" when non-logical obstacles (politics, resource constraints, personality conflicts) are preventing progress -- treat these as separate TRIZ problems to be solved, not as reasons to give up.
- Use "start with what's good and bad about one specific bad solution" when the problem feels too abstract -- contradictions do not exist in the abstract, they exist in concrete solutions. One bad solution gives you the contradiction to solve, which opens the door to completely different solutions.
- Use "the nine-dots test as a mental check" when you feel stuck -- ask yourself whether you have unconsciously drawn a box around the problem. Explicitly list every constraint you are assuming and challenge each one: is this real or imagined?

## Anti-patterns

- **Starting with a pragmatic, constrained attitude**: believing "this is impossible" or seeking the smallest change first limits the solution space before you have explored it. Start by listing everything you want, then add constraints.
- **Ugly Baby Wars**: when team members debate the pros and cons of one specific solution early on, losing sight of the big picture. Avoid this by capturing all solutions in the Bad Solution Park and moving on -- analysis comes later.
- **Rejecting wacky solutions**: dismissing improbable ideas (e.g., Inventive Principle 13 "do it the other way round") as impractical. The ketchup bottle with the opening on the bottom seemed wacky until it became standard -- the wild ideas often contain seeds of genius.
- **Working on someone's bad solution to a deeper problem**: improving a system that is itself an imperfect fix for the real underlying issue. Always check scope with the Ideal Outcome before committing to a problem level.
- **Giving up when a solution keeps failing**: instead of abandoning it, map its flaws with Function Analysis, identify contradictions, and apply the solution tools iteratively. TRIZ assumes all solutions can be improved.
- **Excluding implementers from problem-solving sessions**: people who see problems everywhere are exactly who you need in a TRIZ session -- they identify issues you can address upfront rather than discovering them during implementation.

## Worked Example

The author describes a problem-solving session with a highways maintenance supplier managing a complex annual programme of engineering schemes:

1. The team wanted an ideal works programme that encompassed everything, was totally rigid, and was always adhered to. But in reality, unplanned maintenance repairs required reactive working.

2. They recognized the contradiction: the programme needed to be both **rigid** AND **flexible**.

3. Using the Problem-Solving Table, they identified:
   - **What's Good** (about fixed agreed programmes): everyone knows what is happening when; better resource management; cost certainty. This maps to Parameter 13: Stability of the object's composition.
   - **What's Bad**: difficult to react to unplanned events; not enough information for fixing dates; missed opportunities. This maps to Parameter 35: Adaptability or versatility.

4. Looking up this contradiction in the Technical Contradiction Matrix suggested **Inventive Principle 35: Parameter Change**.

5. This principle suggested establishing varying degrees of flexibility for different projects -- casting large, complex projects in stone and increasing flexibility as projects become smaller. The team found this both highly innovative for their organization and relatively easy to implement.

The author emphasizes that this single contradiction, found in one "bad solution" (a fixed programme), opened the door to a breakthrough.

## Key Takeaways
1. Always start problem solving with a positive attitude -- believe improvement is possible and you will achieve everything you want. The process will handle the inevitable problems later.
2. Call all initial ideas "Bad Solutions" -- this lowers the psychological barrier to sharing and acknowledges that every idea is imperfect but contains useful information.
3. A contradiction does not exist in abstract desires but in a specific solution -- take your first bad solution, identify what is good and bad about it, and use that to look up Inventive Principles.
4. Bad Solution Park ensures equal contribution: go around asking each person for one solution at a time, so the quiet voices are heard alongside the loud ones.
5. Combine solutions through hybridization -- the best result often emerges from merging the plus points of several individual ideas, not from picking one winner.
6. When non-logical barriers block you (politics, resources, personalities), reframe each barrier as a separate TRIZ problem to solve.
7. TRIZ provides both free thinking and structured process -- people who resist "creative" methods can relax knowing their wild thinking occurs within rigorous, systematic steps.
