# Chapter 1: Going from Zero to TRIZ

## Core Idea
TRIZ "bottles" the fundamental logic of problem solving across all disciplines by capturing patterns in how inventive people have solved contradictions -- so you can reapply their clever solutions instead of reinventing the wheel.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Ideality Equation**: `Ideality = Benefits / (Costs + Harms)`
  - Benefits = all outputs you want, expressed as outcomes (NOT solutions)
  - Costs = all inputs required (money, time, materials, energy, effort)
  - Harms = all outputs you do NOT want (waste heat, noise, unused complexity, risk)
  - When to use: at the start of any problem-solving effort to keep focus on outcomes, not premature solutions; also as an ongoing compass to check whether proposed changes actually improve the system.

- **The Prism of TRIZ**: a structured journey from specific problem to general problem to general solution to specific solution.
  - When to use: any time you are stuck and need to break out of domain-specific thinking.
  - How: (1) strip detail to create a conceptual model of your problem, (2) look up how others have solved that type of problem using one of the four solution lists, (3) translate the conceptual solution back into a practical, real-world solution using your domain knowledge.

- **TRIZ Problem-Solving Process** (six generic stages):
  1. Understand and scope the problem
  2. Uncover all needs and scope the solution
  3. Zoom in and define the problem
  4. Identify the solution triggers
  5. Generate solutions to the problem
  6. Rank solutions and implement

## Key Concepts
- **System**: any product, process, or service created and used to meet a need. In TRIZ the term is deliberately broad.
- **Ideality**: the ratio of a system's benefits to its costs plus harms. All TRIZ tools exist to increase Ideality.
- **Benefit**: an outcome you want -- deliberately expressed without describing HOW to achieve it, to avoid locking into a solution too early.
- **Harms**: all outputs you do not want, including "neutral" ones like heat from a laptop or noise from a washing machine. Harms are things you are fundamentally paying for.
- **Prism of TRIZ**: the process of moving between the real/concrete world and the abstract/conceptual world to find and reapply proven solutions.
- **Concept vs. Idea**: a concept is a general way of doing something; an idea is a specific way of putting that concept into practice.
- **Bad Solutions**: a deliberate framing trick -- call all initial ideas "bad solutions" to create a safe space for half-formed or wacky ideas, making it easier for everyone to share without ego.
- **The Four Solution Tools**: the 40 Inventive Principles, the 8 Trends of Technical Evolution, the 76 Standard Solutions, and the Effects Database -- collectively contain approximately 100 conceptual solutions distilled from patent analysis.

## Mental Models
- **Start with what you want, not what you have.** Most people tweak the current system; TRIZ insists you first define all ideal outcomes, then identify what problems stand in the way.
- **Use the Prism of TRIZ when stuck in domain-specific thinking.** Move your problem into the abstract, find which general-problem category it fits, and access solutions from entirely different industries.
- **Treat every problem as an opportunity to increase Ideality.** When something goes wrong or someone says "that won't work because...", you have just identified either a cost to reduce or a harm to eliminate.
- **Use Idea-Concept thinking when you run dry in brainstorming.** Capture the concepts behind each existing idea, then use each concept as a trigger to generate a new wave of ideas.

## Anti-patterns
- **Falling in love with your first solution**: the Ideality equation forces you to keep benefits (not solutions) front and center. If you fixate on a solution, you stop looking for potentially better ones.
- **Accepting compromise as inevitable**: TRIZ rejects the "pick one or find a middle ground" reflex. Contradictions only exist in current solution approaches -- find a different approach.
- **Assuming you lack the expertise to solve a problem**: TRIZ cannot replace domain knowledge, but it directs you to where to look. Involve people with general domain knowledge even if they lack deep expertise in the specific problem.
- **Treating ideas as rare treasures to hoard**: holding onto an idea blocks further thinking. Share freely; the more you give away, the more you receive from others.

## Worked Example
To demonstrate how different disciplines approach the same problem, the author uses the "glass of water" icebreaker: place a glass of water on a table and ask people to remove the water without moving the glass or the table.

- Mechanical engineers do things to the glass (break it, drill a hole, vibrate it)
- Chemists do things to the water (hydrolysis to split into hydrogen and oxygen, chemical transformation)
- Biologists use living things (a person drinks it with a straw, flowers draw up the water)
- A parent suggested leaving a toddler alone for two minutes ("you'll come back and the water will be all over the floor")

TRIZ helps capture the knowledge of ALL these perspectives and go beyond them to find novel solutions none would have considered alone.

## Key Takeaways
1. Ideality (`Benefits / (Costs + Harms)`) is your north star -- use it to evaluate every solution and keep your focus on outcomes.
2. Problems repeat across industries; if you can model your problem generically, you can reapply someone else's clever solution.
3. The Prism of TRIZ (specific problem -> general problem -> general solution -> specific solution) is the core mental move that unlocks all TRIZ tools.
4. Domain knowledge is irreplaceable -- TRIZ directs your expertise and creativity, it does not substitute for them.
5. Expand your problem-solving team beyond deep experts; people with general domain knowledge using TRIZ can generate highly innovative and practical solutions.
6. Cultivate a "can do" attitude: any roadblock is just another problem you can solve with TRIZ, which builds persistence and a restless drive to improve things.
7. Share ideas freely. Treating ideas as cheap and abundant actually makes them more abundant.
