# Chapter 7: Breaking Psychological Inertia with the TRIZ Creativity Tools

## Core Idea
Psychological inertia -- being stuck in a mental rut shaped by assumptions, expertise, and habits -- is the single biggest block to creativity. TRIZ provides explicit tools (Smart Little People, Size-Time-Cost, and 11 others) to recognise this inertia and systematically break out of it, allowing you to think creatively on demand.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Smart Little People (SLP)**: a conceptual modelling tool where you imagine the problem zone as populated by tiny, intelligent, infinitely numerous "people" who can do or be anything -- helpful ones solve problems, naughty ones cause them
  - When to use: when you are stuck on a physical or technical problem and need a fresh perspective, or when you need to model exactly what is happening at a detailed level
  - How: (1) Draw a picture of the current system or problem area, (2) Model how it functions (and fails) using Smart Little People -- show what the naughty people do and what the helpful people do, (3) Imagine how additional helpful little people could arrive and solve the problems, (4) Translate the conceptual SLP solutions back into real-world practical solutions using your expertise

- **Size-Time-Cost**: a thinking-stretching tool that forces you to consider extreme values of the parameters that most commonly constrain your thinking
  - When to use: when you suspect your assumptions about constraints (budget, timeframe, scale) are limiting your solution space
  - How: for each parameter (Size, Time, Cost -- and optionally others like number of staff), imagine both extremes: infinitely large AND infinitely small / forever AND no time / infinite budget AND zero budget. Generate solutions for each extreme, then work out how to bring the most promising ideas back to practical reality

- **Bad Solution Park**: a technique for non-judgemental idea capture
  - When to use: during any creative thinking session, individually or in teams
  - How: name every idea a "bad solution" to acknowledge it is imperfect and can be improved. Capture all ideas immediately without judgement. Bad solutions break psychological inertia because they tell you something you want, even if you do not yet know how to implement it.

## Key Concepts
- **Psychological Inertia**: the TRIZ term (borrowed from physics) for being stuck in a particular way of thinking -- your mental "object" keeps moving in the same direction at the same speed unless an external force acts upon it. It manifests as assumptions about the problem, the solution, constraints, what can be changed, and which solutions are viable.
- **Assumptions (as inertia source)**: the first rule of assumptions is that you do not know you are making them. They define and narrow your thinking so you no longer see the bigger picture. Types include assumptions about problem difficulty, solution nature, time/money constraints, mental images, and what is possible to change.
- **Unconscious Competence (the creativity trap)**: when professional expertise becomes second nature, your brain takes efficient shortcuts based on past experience. This is useful for routine tasks but actively blocks creative thinking because it is hard-wired to repeat past success, not discover novel solutions.
- **The "Aha!" Moment (TRIZ interpretation)**: the experience of insight is typically the realisation that you have been making an assumption, combined with discovering a solution that breaks that assumption. TRIZ tools create these moments systematically rather than waiting for them to happen.
- **Thinking in Extremes**: a fundamental TRIZ philosophy -- go to a far-out, wacky place to restructure your view of the problem, test whether constraints are real, and gain a new perspective, then work out how to bring ideas back to practical reality.

## Mental Models
- Use "I am probably in psychological inertia right now" as your default assumption when problem-solving -- the answer to "Am I experiencing psychological inertia?" is almost always yes. This humility opens the door to the creativity tools.
- Use the "constraints are hypotheses" mindset when someone says "You can't do that because..." -- every objection is an assumption that can be tested. Just because you do not know how to do something does not mean it is hard; it may be trivially easy for someone else.
- Use "Bad Solution Park" when you feel the urge to dismiss an idea -- instead of killing it, park it. Bad solutions break inertia because they reveal something you want; the reason they seem impractical may simply be that you do not yet know the implementation path (but someone else might).
- Use "zoom into the problem zone" (SLP) when you are stuck on a physical mechanism -- imagining yourself and countless helpers at the exact location of the problem forces you to model the fine detail and generates solutions that high-level thinking would miss.

## Anti-patterns
- **Premature judgement**: testing and picking apart ideas as they are generated. This slows thinking and discourages creativity. The luxury of playing with extreme, impractical ideas allows the brain to work most effectively because you are not constantly second-guessing yourself.
- **Expertise as a creativity blocker**: the more experience you have, the stronger your gut feeling toward trusted known solutions becomes, and the less flexible your thinking. The good news is that more expertise also means more creative potential -- *if* you explicitly apply creative thinking techniques.
- **Dismissing "wacky" ideas**: an idea that seems completely impractical may simply be one you do not know how to implement. During a real workshop, the suggestion to "buy a plane" (impossible on the budget) led to "rent a plane" (practical and cheap) -- one of five solutions taken forward.
- **Fighting psychological inertia instead of understanding it**: psychological inertia exists for good reason -- it enables efficient routine thinking and frees mental space for higher-level problems. The goal is not to eliminate it but to recognise when it is limiting you and override it deliberately.

## Worked Example
**Redesigning a garlic press using Smart Little People:**

Problem: Traditional garlic presses are easy to use but fiddly and difficult to clean. You want a garlic press that is easier to clean.

Step 1 -- Draw the current system/problem area.

Step 2 -- Model it with Smart Little People: imagine the garlic press people pushing garlic flesh and juice people through holes. The problem is that garlic flesh and juice people get stuck in the holes and on the press surfaces -- they do not want to leave.

Step 3 -- Imagine helpful little people arriving to solve the problem:
- The garlic press people could *push* the garlic flesh and juice people away
- New helpful people could arrive with *brushes* and sweep away the stuck garlic
- The garlic press people could *chop up* the garlic flesh people with swords

Step 4 -- Translate to real-world solutions:
- "Pushing away" becomes a slippery coating like Teflon on the press surface, making it hard for garlic to attach and easy to clean
- "Brushes" becomes a separate cleaning part with prongs (e.g., silicone) that slot into the holes and clean them, but do not get dirty themselves
- "Swords" suggests slicing rather than crushing garlic -- pressing it against sharp blades that are flat and easy to rinse clean, possibly combined with the Teflon-like coating idea

### Worked Example 2: Reducing office energy use with Size-Time-Cost

- **Size -- Infinitely large**: build our own power station; improve whole-building insulation; move office to a more temperate region
- **Size -- Infinitely small**: change office temperature by season and ask everyone to dress appropriately; manage door/window openings; do not heat/cool all parts of the office
- **Time -- Forever**: design a new building requiring less energy; conduct a full analysis to discover the source of most losses
- **Time -- No time**: adjust thermostat by a couple of degrees; move equipment and people to manage heat better; switch to energy-efficient bulbs
- **Cost -- Infinite budget**: buy/build a new building; invest in self-powered equipment research; apply for grants for renewable energy (e.g., solar panels)
- **Cost -- No budget**: ensure equipment is turned off (not on standby); remove unnecessary energy-consuming equipment; ensure lights are off when rooms are unused

## Key Takeaways
1. Psychological inertia is universal and largely invisible: assume you have it, then use TRIZ tools to challenge it explicitly.
2. Bad solutions are essential: they break inertia, tell you something you want, and often contain the seed of a practical solution once someone else's knowledge is added.
3. Smart Little People works because empathy plus infinite replaceability equals fearless creativity: you can imagine dangerous or destructive solutions without protective instinct blocking them.
4. Size-Time-Cost works because extremes restructure your view: imagining zero budget reveals things you could simply stop doing; imagining infinite budget reveals what you truly want.
5. Creativity is a habit you can build: be curious, try new things, challenge constraints, make time for thinking, do not give up, think big, be positive.
6. Professional expertise is both your greatest asset and your greatest creativity blocker: use TRIZ tools to temporarily suspend your automatic expert judgement and explore the full solution space.
