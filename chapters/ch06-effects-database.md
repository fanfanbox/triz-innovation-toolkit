# Chapter 6: Using the TRIZ Effects Database

## Core Idea
Someone else has probably already solved your problem. The TRIZ Effects Database reorganises all known scientific effects by what they *do* (their function), so you can look up answers to "How do I...?" questions and reapply proven solutions from any field to your own.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Prism of TRIZ**: a systematic process of abstracting your specific problem to a conceptual problem, finding known conceptual solutions, then translating them back into practical solutions for your context
  - When to use: any time you are problem-solving and want to access knowledge beyond your own experience
  - How: (1) Strip out unnecessary detail and technical jargon from your real-world problem to reach a conceptual problem, (2) Search for analogous conceptual solutions (via the Effects Database, analogy search, or other TRIZ tools), (3) Translate those conceptual solutions back into real-world solutions for your specific situation

- **The X-Factor**: a thinking tool that defines what you are looking for as a simple Subject-Action-Object statement, where the Subject is the unknown thing you are hunting for
  - When to use: whenever you need a function delivered but do not know how to achieve it
  - How: define the function as "Something (X) does [action] to [object]" -- for example, "X cleans a solid," "X constrains a gas," "X measures temperature" -- then search the Effects Database or wider world for that function

- **The Effects Database**: a catalogue of all known scientific and engineering effects, reorganised by application (what they do) rather than by scientific discipline
  - When to use: when you have a technical problem and need to know what physical phenomena can deliver a specific function or change a specific parameter
  - How: (1) Describe your problem as a "How do I...?" question, (2) Define an X-Factor as a function or parameter change, (3) Look up that function in the database, (4) Translate conceptual solutions into real-world implementations

## Key Concepts
- **Simple Language**: stripping out all technical jargon, acronyms, and discipline-specific terminology so that a problem can be understood by anyone. This is the essential first step of the Prism of TRIZ -- if you cannot explain a problem to a six-year-old, you do not really understand it yourself (Feynman's test).
- **Analogous Thinking**: finding solutions by recognising that your problem is conceptually similar to problems others have solved in different industries or fields. The similarities are at the conceptual level; the differences are in the detail.
- **Innovation (TRIZ definition)**: reapplying existing, proven solutions in new applications. Innovation is not inventing something from nothing; it is seeing how a solution from one domain can be transferred to another.
- **Psychological Inertia through Language**: the specific jargon of your discipline can lock you into a particular solution-space and prevent you from seeing cross-domain analogies. Using simple, general language breaks this inertia.
- **Life and Death Analogies**: the best place to look for analogous solutions is in industries where your specific problem is a matter of life and death -- they will have invested the most in finding robust solutions.
- **Invention (TRIZ definition)**: matching currently unmet needs with existing technologies, either by applying old technologies in new ways or by combining existing technologies to create something novel.

## Mental Models
- Use the "strip to the conceptual" discipline when you catch yourself using jargon -- rephrase the problem using only the most basic words. This alone often reveals that you do not fully understand the problem or that you are assuming a particular solution.
- Use the "X-Factor search" when you have a clear function gap -- instead of brainstorming solutions, ask "What already exists in the world that can [action] [object]?" and search systematically rather than randomly.
- Use the "follow the money" heuristic for analogy search when life-and-death is too narrow -- look to industries with massive R&D budgets (aerospace, Formula 1, pharmaceuticals) that have already invested heavily in solving similar functional problems.
- Use the "go cheap" lens when seeking elegant, streamlined solutions -- commodity products (pre-packaged food, cleaning products, toys) have been relentlessly optimised to deliver their functions at minimal cost; their solutions are often brilliantly simple.

## Anti-patterns
- **Reinventing the wheel**: jumping straight to generating your own solutions without first checking whether an existing, proven solution already exists. The first and most efficient step is always to ask "Has someone else solved a similar problem?"
- **Hiding behind jargon**: using technical language as a shield when you do not fully understand the situation. If you cannot explain a problem in words a child would understand, your understanding is incomplete, and you will search for solutions in the wrong places.
- **Domain-locked search**: only looking for solutions within your own industry or discipline. A mechanical engineer will suggest mechanical solutions; a chemist will suggest chemical ones. The Effects Database gives you access to *all* of them.
- **Confusing the tool with the database itself**: the Effects Database is specifically for technical problems. For non-technical problems, use the same Prism-of-TRIZ logic with analogy search strategies (life and death, follow the money, go cheap) instead.

## Worked Example
**Cleaning a car windscreen using the Effects Database:**

Step 1 -- Describe the problem in normal language: "Clean my car windscreen."

Step 2 -- Define the X-Factor: the function needed is "X-Factor cleans solid." In Subject-Action-Object terms, this means "Something cleans the solid (windscreen)."

Step 3 -- Look up "clean a solid" in the Effects Database. The Oxford Creativity database returns approximately 90 solutions, including: abrasion, acoustic cavitation, adsorption, brush, capillary action, catalysis, cavitation, combustion, electrolysis, enzyme, erosion, fan, fermentation, ferromagnetism, filter, fluid spray, friction, froth floatation, ion exchange, jet, laser, laser ablation, lotus leaf effect, magnetic field, mechanical force, molecular sieve, oxidation, ozone, phase change, plasma, pyrolysis, radiation, resonance, sonochemistry, sorption, sound, sponge, sublimation, suction, supercritical fluid, surfactant, ultrasonic vibration, vacuum, vibration, wear, weathering, and many more.

Step 4 -- Translate to real-world solutions:
- *Solutions you would have thought of anyway*: fluid spray and jet (conventional washing), surfactants and amphiphiles (detergents)
- *Solutions you knew about but forgot*: lotus leaf effect (self-cleaning glass technology)
- *Solutions beyond your expertise*: ultrasonic vibration (already used in toothbrushes), laser ablation, cavitation, electrolysis, plasma -- each suggesting a completely different approach to windscreen cleaning that a mechanical engineer or chemist working alone would likely never consider

The power of the database is that it gives you *all* the ways the world knows to clean a solid, not just the ones your discipline would suggest.

## Key Takeaways
1. The Prism of TRIZ is your universal problem-solving workflow: strip to the conceptual, find known conceptual solutions, translate back to your reality. This applies whether you use the Effects Database or analogy search.
2. Simple language is a superpower: removing jargon forces you to truly understand your problem and opens the door to solutions from any field.
3. The X-Factor transforms vague needs into searchable functions: "I need something that does X to Y" is a query, not a wish.
4. The Effects Database gives you three categories of solutions: the ones you would have found anyway, the ones you knew but forgot, and -- most valuable -- the ones completely beyond your knowledge.
5. Innovation is reapplying proven solutions in new contexts: you do not need to invent new science; you need to connect existing science to new problems.
6. For non-technical problems, use analogy search strategies: look to life-and-death industries, high-R&D sectors, or commoditised products for the most advanced or most elegant solutions.
