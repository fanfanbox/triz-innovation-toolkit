# Chapter 5: Improving Ideality by Using Resources

## Core Idea
Ideality is the ratio of benefits to costs and harms. The most elegant way to improve any system is by using resources you already have -- getting what you want without adding anything new, and ideally converting harmful outputs into benefits.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Ideality Equation**: Ideality = Benefits / (Costs + Harms)
  - When to use: whenever choosing between competing systems, evaluating a current system, or identifying where to focus improvement efforts
  - How: list all benefits (outcomes you want), all costs (inputs required), and all harms (outputs you don't want); rank systems by their overall Ideality ratio

- **Benefits-Functions-Solutions Chain**: Benefits (what you want) are delivered by Functions (how you get it), which are delivered by specific Solutions (features/resources)
  - When to use: when you need to find alternative ways to achieve a desired outcome
  - How: move up by asking "Why?" (from solution to function to benefit); move down by asking "How?" (from benefit to function to solution)

- **Resource Hunting Process**: a structured method for solving problems using only what is already available
  - When to use: any problem where adding new components is costly, undesirable, or impossible (closed systems, regulated industries, tight budgets)
  - How: (1) Identify the benefit(s) you want, (2) Identify the function(s) that can deliver them, (3) Go through resource prompts and list all resources, (4) Identify which resources can deliver the functions you want

## Key Concepts
- **Benefit**: an outcome or result you want from your system -- describes *what* you want, not *how* you get it. The *prime benefit* is the single main thing your system must deliver.
- **Cost**: any input required to deliver a function -- includes money, time, design effort, energy for manufacture, maintenance, space, and all resources going into system creation and operation.
- **Harm**: any output from or function in your system that you do not want. TRIZ is brutal here: neutral outputs do not exist. If it is not a benefit, it is a harm. Includes obvious harms (noise, pollution) and subtle ones (unused features, complexity).
- **Resource**: anything available in or around your system, already there, usable for free, and close to the problem -- including environmental elements (sunlight, gravity, ambient temperature), system features (shape, size, color, transparency), sub-system components, time, space, voids (empty spaces), and even harmful outputs or waste.
- **Function**: the *how* that delivers a benefit. A specific function is described as a Subject-Action-Object (something doing something to something else).
- **Ideality**: a measure of a system's "goodness" -- not a real equation but a ratio balancing all benefits against all costs and all harms. Different stakeholders may value different things, so Ideality varies by perspective.

## Mental Models
- Use the "Why-How" ladder when you are stuck on a specific solution -- climb up by asking "Why do I want this?" to discover the underlying benefit, then descend by asking "How else could I deliver that benefit?" to find alternative functions and solutions.
- Use the "everything is a resource" lens when facing a problem with a tight constraint -- time, space, voids, shape, gravity, emotions, knowledge, competitive spirit, and even harmful outputs can all deliver useful functions for free.
- Use the "harm-to-benefit conversion" mindset when you uncover a negative output -- instead of merely tolerating it, ask how it could be redirected to serve a useful purpose (like converting kitchen waste to compost, or cork chippings to energy).
- Use the "Ideality as compass" when making any decision between alternatives -- list the benefits, costs, and harms of each option, then choose the one with the highest overall Ideality, remembering that different stakeholders may weight factors differently.

## Anti-patterns
- **Adding things to solve problems**: the traditional reflex is to throw more components, money, or complexity at a problem. This adds costs and often introduces new harms. Always first ask whether something already in or around the system can provide the needed function.
- **Confusing features with benefits**: describing what you want in terms of a specific solution ("a big car") rather than the underlying benefit ("status" or "capacity to transport grandchildren") locks you into one implementation and blinds you to better alternatives.
- **Ignoring idle time and empty space**: forgetting that time gaps in a process and voids in a physical system are resources ready to be exploited means missing some of the most elegant, cost-free solutions available.
- **Accepting extra functions as "free"**: every additional function you do not need is a harm -- it adds complexity, confusion, cost, and processing overhead. Insist on getting only what you actually want.

## Worked Example
**The Titanic survival problem** (adapted from Ellen Domb's teaching exercise):

Context: The Titanic hit an iceberg carrying 2,224 people with lifeboats for only 1,178. The rescue ship was four hours away; the ship would sink in two hours. The chief naval architect was on board, so everyone knew the timeline.

Step 1 -- Benefit wanted: Everybody survives. But people die in cold water within four minutes and drowning is also a risk.

Step 2 -- Functions wanted: Stay afloat; stay warm.

Step 3 -- Resource inventory:
- *Knowledge/experience*: chief naval architect, engineers, disciplined crew, passengers' experience
- *Super-system/environment*: good weather (still water, no wind), speed of boat, other rescue boat
- *System features*: the ship itself
- *Sub-system/components*: lifeboats, buoyancy aids, deckchairs, musical instruments, bedding, cooking equipment, bottles, baths, luggage, unflooded bulkheads, working engines, heating, ventilation system, winches, cranes
- *Time*: two hours before sinking; need a solution lasting only two more hours
- *Space*: rescue ship crew reachable via telegraph
- *Harms/waste*: the iceberg itself

Step 4 -- Solutions from resources:
1. Organise passengers to build more flotation devices in the two available hours from buoyant component resources
2. Make holes in bulkheads on the other side so the ship sinks evenly instead of breaking in two, buying enough time for rescue
3. Move everyone onto the iceberg
4. Overload the lifeboats -- they were designed for high winds and rough seas; in calm weather, possibly everyone could have fit and survived

## Key Takeaways
1. Ideality is your compass: every TRIZ problem-solving move should increase the ratio of benefits to costs-plus-harms. If it does not, you are moving in the wrong direction.
2. Resources are everywhere: time, space, voids, shape, gravity, knowledge, competitive spirit, waste, environmental conditions -- everything around you can be used for free if you learn to see it.
3. Separate benefits from functions from solutions: asking "Why?" moves you up to more conceptual thinking; asking "How?" moves you down to concrete options. Every step uncovers alternative paths.
4. Harms are opportunities in disguise: any harmful output can potentially be converted into a useful input somewhere in your system or in a neighboring system.
5. Start with what you want, not what you think is possible: defining benefits first keeps you focused on elegant, minimal solutions rather than bloated, expensive ones.
6. Resource thinking should become a reflex: regularly ask "How can I get what I want using what I've already got?" before adding anything new.
