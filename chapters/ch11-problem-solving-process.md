# Chapter 11: Applying the TRIZ Problem-Solving Process

## Core Idea
The Oxford TRIZ process distills the rigorous logic of ARIZ into a faster, more flexible, six-stage route map that guides teams from a messy problem to ranked, implementable solutions through systematic divergence and convergence cycles.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Oxford TRIZ Problem-Solving Process (Six Stages)**:
  - When to use: for any difficult problem, especially team-based problems requiring both thorough understanding and creative solutions. Adaptable for sessions from 2-4 hours (team) to shorter solo work.
  - How: Stage 1 -- Understand and scope the problem (9 Boxes context map, Ideality Audit). Stage 2 -- Uncover all needs and scope the solution (Ideal Outcome, Ideal System, 9 Boxes). Stage 3 -- Define the problem (Function Analysis, Technical Contradiction Matrix, Separation Principles, Smart Little People). Stage 4 -- Identify solution triggers and generate solutions (Standard Solutions, Trimming, 40 Principles, Trends, Effects Database, Smart Little People, Size-Time-Cost, Resources/X-Factor, 9 Boxes). Stage 5 -- Rank solutions and implement (Ideality Plot). Stage 6 (optional) -- Improve solutions by iterating.

- **Bad Solution Park (Stage 0 / Continuous)**:
  - When to use: from the very beginning and throughout the entire process.
  - How: create a space where all top-of-head ideas are captured as "Bad Solutions" -- acknowledging they are imperfect but contain useful information. This allows people to release their preconceived solutions and focus on the process. Review the Park at the generate-solutions stage.

- **Ideality Plot (Solution Ranking)**:
  - When to use: after generating many solutions, to converge them into a manageable set with clear next actions.
  - How: plot each solution on a matrix of Benefits (high/low) vs. Costs+Harms (low/high). Four quadrants emerge:
    - **Implement** (high benefits, low costs/harms): do now.
    - **Solve Problems** (high benefits, high costs/harms): the most exciting -- apply contradiction-solving and Standard Solutions to reduce the downsides. These become long-term solutions.
    - **Improve** (low benefits, low costs/harms): easy but low-impact. Drive up the Trends, solve insufficiencies, or combine with other solutions.
    - **Park for Now** (low benefits, high costs/harms): deprioritize; investigate only if you have time and better options are exhausted.

- **Convergence by Concept**:
  - When to use: when you still have too many solutions after the Ideality Plot ranking.
  - How: group solutions by their underlying concept (the fundamental idea, not the specific implementation). This reveals the best broad directions forward while preserving multiple specific ways to move in each direction.

## Key Concepts

- **Oxford TRIZ Process**: a simplified, flexible alternative to ARIZ that bottles the logic of inventive problem solving into six manageable stages. Easier to learn, faster to apply, and less risky than ARIZ (which has one critical decision point where a wrong choice wastes all prior work).
- **ARIZ**: Altshuller's original Algorithm of Inventive Problem Solving -- a long, rigorous, many-step process powerful for the hardest problems but overkill for simpler ones and daunting for novices.
- **Prime Output**: the main thing your system exists to deliver -- e.g., a restaurant's Prime Output is "the provision of delicious food and drink." Always keep this in mind during detailed problem solving, so you do not lose sight of the system's fundamental purpose.
- **Divergence**: the phase of problem solving where you generate many possibilities -- exploring needs, understanding context, brainstorming solutions. Requires open, generative thinking.
- **Convergence**: the phase where you narrow options -- distilling needs into a defined Ideal Outcome, defining the problem precisely, ranking and selecting solutions. Requires critical, evaluative thinking.
- **Thinking Fast**: intuitive leaps, flashes of inspiration, largely unconscious processing. Valuable but unreliable because it is subject to bias (psychological inertia). Allow it to happen but do not rely on it.
- **Thinking Slow**: systematic, step-by-step reasoning through the problem space and solution space. Covers the whole territory, breaks psychological inertia, and ensures nothing is missed. The TRIZ process embodies this.
- **Stage-Gate Integration**: incorporating TRIZ into an existing stage-gate product development process -- at any gate where a problem is found (concept, design, prototype, manufacturing, supply chain), apply TRIZ to solve it before proceeding.
- **Wicked Problems / Messy Problems**: problems that start as a confusing tangle of interconnected issues, where understanding the problem is as important as finding solutions. Function Analysis (Stage 3) is essential for these.

## Mental Models

- Use "sharpening the axe before cutting" when tempted to jump straight to solutions -- spending the first half of a session on problem understanding (defining needs, understanding the current system, finding gaps) is not wasted time; it is what makes the solution-generation phase productive rather than random.
- Use "allow ideas at every stage, but write them down and let them go" when ideas arise during problem understanding phases -- do not suppress inspiration, but capture it in the Bad Solution Park and return focus to the current stage. The process will generate even better ideas when you reach the solution stage.
- Use "the funnel metaphor" to explain the value of the early stages to teams who prefer brainstorming -- the first stages funnel scattered thinking into focused, well-defined problems so creative energy is directed at the highest-value targets.
- Use "diverge then converge at each stage" to manage team anxiety -- when ideas are diverging (seemingly out of control), reassure the team that the next stage will converge them again. The process is cyclical: diverge (explore needs) -> converge (define Ideal), diverge (generate solutions) -> converge (rank by Ideality), diverge (implement/improve) -> converge (final solution).

## Anti-patterns

- **Generating solutions to the wrong problem**: the more solutions you generate for a poorly defined problem, the farther you go from where you need to be. Always complete problem understanding stages first.
- **Defining the problem before defining the Ideal**: this creates psychological inertia by anchoring your thinking to the current system, encouraging incremental changes rather than innovative step-changes. Define the Ideal Outcome (Stage 2) before the problem (Stage 3).
- **Rejecting solutions based on their problems**: the natural instinct is to dismiss high-benefit, high-problem solutions. But those are the most exciting -- their problems can be solved with TRIZ, and competitors may reject the same solutions for the same reason, giving you an advantage.
- **Omitting implementers from the session**: people who have to implement solutions need to be in the room. They will identify problems you can address upfront, and they will feel ownership of the solutions, making implementation smoother.
- **Trying to reverse-engineer intuition into the process**: if you have a flash of inspiration that does not seem to fit the TRIZ process, do not waste time forcing it into the framework. The process has shaped your thinking; accept the solution and move on. The goal is solving problems, not being a TRIZ purist.
- **Short-circuiting stages without showing your work**: teams will resist if you jump to presenting a contradiction without explaining the logic that got you there. Walk through each stage so everyone understands and trusts the reasoning.

## Worked Example

The author describes a three-day facilitated TRIZ workshop:

On day one, the team worked through problem understanding and scoping stages, ultimately defining the core contradiction they faced. At the start of day two, the facilitators presented the contradiction and asked the team to solve it. The team refused to engage -- they needed to see the logic behind the choice. Once the facilitators "showed their workings" -- explaining how each preceding step led to that specific contradiction -- the team trusted the process and engaged fully.

This demonstrates that the process itself creates buy-in. Skipping steps, even to save time, undermines the trust that makes team problem solving effective.

The author also references Amazon's Kindle vs. Sony Reader as a business example of TRIZ-like thinking: Amazon succeeded not by having superior hardware (Sony's Reader was lighter, smaller, had a better screen) but by solving problems for both customers AND suppliers. Customers needed easy access and payment for e-books; publishers feared piracy. Amazon's closed system and one-stop shop solved both problems simultaneously, while Sony addressed only the reader device itself. This is the TRIZ principle of looking at the broader system and solving other people's problems to enable your solution to thrive.

## Key Takeaways
1. Never jump to generating solutions without understanding the problem -- the more solutions you generate for the wrong problem, the farther away from your goal you go.
2. Define the Ideal Outcome before defining the problem -- otherwise, the current system anchors your thinking and limits you to incremental changes.
3. The Ideality Plot gives you not just a ranking but a prescription: implement (easy wins), solve problems (high-impact long-term), improve (low-impact quick fixes), park (discard).
4. Allow divergent and convergent thinking at every stage -- reassure teams that divergence is always followed by convergence; the process is cyclical, not chaotic.
5. Write down every idea that comes during analysis phases (in the Bad Solution Park) and let it go -- this frees mental space to focus on the current stage.
6. Involve the people who will implement solutions in the problem-solving session -- they see problems you miss and they will champion solutions they helped create.
7. Improve solutions by running them through the process again -- time is cheap relative to the value of a more elegant, robust solution.
