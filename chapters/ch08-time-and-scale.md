# Chapter 8: Thinking in Time and Scale

## Core Idea
Naturally creative people instinctively zoom in and out of a problem -- examining the detail, the big picture, the past, and the future. The 9-Boxes (or 9-Windows) tool captures this thinking pattern so anyone can systematically stretch their view of a situation across time and scale, finding innovative solutions and ensuring they solve the right problem.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The 9-Boxes (9-Windows)**: a 3x3 grid mapping three levels of scale (super-system, system, sub-system) against three time periods (past, present, future)
  - When to use: for understanding problems (context mapping), generating solutions (solution mapping), identifying all problem causes (hazard mapping), locating resources (resource mapping), defining ideal systems, capturing requirements, and strategic planning
  - How: draw a 3x3 grid. Columns = time (past, present, future). Rows = scale (super-system above, system in middle, sub-system below). Fill every box systematically, never leaving any blank. Define your time and scale boundaries explicitly before starting.

- **Context Map**: a 9-Boxes variant used at the beginning of problem-solving to understand how you arrived in the current situation and where you could end up
  - When to use: early-stage problem exploration, before committing to a solution direction
  - How: fill all nine boxes with what is happening or has happened at each time-and-scale intersection; use this to check that you are solving the right problem at the right level

- **Solution Map**: a 9-Boxes variant used to generate creative solutions across all times and scales
  - When to use: after defining the problem, when you need to generate as many solution ideas as possible
  - How: for each of the nine boxes, ask "What solution could be applied here?" Combine with other TRIZ solution tools (Inventive Principles, Effects Database, Standard Solutions) within each box for even more ideas. Afterwards, check for empty or light boxes to identify blind spots.

- **Problem Causes and Hazards Map**: a 9-Boxes variant for risk analysis
  - When to use: when preventing a serious downside or analysing a complex failure
  - How: map every potential cause of the problem across all nine boxes. Many disasters occur when multiple circumstances combine -- each is a problem cause, and addressing just one may be sufficient to prevent the entire failure.

## Key Concepts
- **Super-system**: everything above or outside your system -- the environment, market structures, regulations, culture, competitors, and anything your system interacts with but does not contain. Zooming out to the super-system often reveals the most elegant solution locations.
- **Sub-system**: all the individual parts, details, components, technologies, and people that come together to make your system. Zooming in to the sub-system reveals root causes and micro-level intervention points.
- **System**: the focal level you have defined -- your product, process, team, or organisation. This is the middle row of the 9-Boxes.
- **Psychological Inertia in Scale**: most people have a fixed, habitual view of at what level a solution should be found. The 9-Boxes explicitly challenges this by forcing you to generate solutions at every level and time period.
- **Time as a Resource**: processes can be shifted in time to exploit idle periods (e.g., using shipping time to change chocolate consistency), problems can be solved before they occur (prevention at past or super-system levels), and post-problem mitigation can convert harms into lesser harms (e.g., flooding your own house with clean water before dirty floodwater arrives).
- **The 7-plus-or-minus-2 Rule**: psychologists suggest people can hold roughly seven (plus or minus two) concepts in working memory. Nine boxes sits right at this cognitive limit -- more than 12 boxes becomes unwieldy; if your situation demands more granularity, use multiple sets of 9-Boxes.

## Mental Models
- Use "Google-Earth your problem" when you feel stuck or overwhelmed by detail -- zoom all the way out to the super-system (what larger forces are at play?), then zoom all the way in to the sub-system (what is happening at the component or molecular level?). The answer rarely lives at the default zoom level.
- Use "solve before it happens" as your first instinct -- before tackling a problem in the present, check the past and super-system boxes. Prevention at the super-system level (regulation, environment design, user behaviour) is often the most elegant and least costly intervention.
- Use "map the perfect storm" when analysing a serious failure -- most disasters are not caused by a single factor. Fill all nine boxes with contributing causes; each one is an independent opportunity to prevent recurrence.
- Use "the solution gap check" after generating ideas -- arrange all your solutions in a 9-Box grid and look for empty boxes. If the super-system column is empty, you are probably making assumptions about what is possible to change. If the future column is empty, you are only thinking short-term.

## Anti-patterns
- **Leaving boxes blank**: an empty box is a missed opportunity or an unexamined assumption. Every box should contain something, even if it feels speculative. Use sticky notes so you can move items later.
- **Solving the wrong problem at the wrong level**: coming up with great solutions to the wrong problem takes you further from where you want to be. Always context-map first to verify you are tackling the problem at the correct scale and time horizon.
- **Getting stuck in one box**: over-focusing on one small detail means you lose sight of the big picture. The discipline of moving systematically through all nine boxes prevents this tunnel vision.
- **Assuming super-system solutions are impossible**: many people generate few ideas in the super-system because they assume regulations, markets, or the environment cannot be changed. This is psychological inertia -- push through it and generate ideas anyway; the constraint may not be as rigid as it appears.
- **Using too many boxes**: if you create 81 boxes, you lose the clarity that makes the tool powerful. If your situation genuinely needs more granularity, use multiple separate 9-Box grids rather than one giant grid.

## Worked Example
**Context mapping a headache at work:**

- **Past Super-system**: office culture of long hours, no breaks
- **Past System**: worked late previous night, skipped meals
- **Past Sub-system**: previous eye strain from old monitor
- **Present Super-system**: open-plan office noise, air conditioning drying air
- **Present System**: sitting at desk for 6 hours straight, poor posture
- **Present Sub-system**: dehydrated (not drinking enough water), eye strain from screen glare, muscle tension in neck
- **Future Super-system**: (if unaddressed) chronic migraines, sick leave, reduced productivity
- **Future System**: (if addressed) regular breaks, ergonomic assessment, hydration routine
- **Future Sub-system**: (if addressed) new monitor, glasses with anti-glare coating, stretching exercises

This mapping reveals that simply taking a painkiller (present sub-system solution) addresses only one box. The real solution portfolio spans hydration, ergonomics, workplace culture, screen equipment, and break routines -- solutions at multiple levels that together prevent the problem.

### Worked Example 2: Barn owl deaths by road traffic

- Multiple causes mapped across all nine boxes -- owls hunt near roads (good prey source), roadside verges are ideal hunting habitat, cars strike them. Solutions appear in every box: change verge management (past super-system), install fencing (present super-system), modify car speeds (present system), develop owl deterrents (present sub-system).

## Key Takeaways
1. The 9-Boxes is not one tool but a family of tools: context maps, solution maps, hazard maps, resource maps, ideal system maps, and requirements maps all use the same 3x3 grid for different purposes.
2. Define your time and scale boundaries explicitly before filling boxes -- the exact boundaries depend on your industry and problem, but always stretch beyond the immediate.
3. Creative people naturally think in time and scale; the 9-Boxes makes this thinking pattern explicit and repeatable for everyone.
4. Solutions exist in every box: if you only generate ideas in the present-system box, you are missing the majority of your solution space.
5. Problems are multi-causal: mapping all contributing factors across nine boxes reveals that addressing just one cause can often prevent the entire failure.
6. Time is a resource, not just a dimension: solving before a problem occurs, exploiting idle time, and mitigating after the worst happens are all legitimate solution strategies.
7. The 9-Boxes supercharges other TRIZ tools: combine it with Inventive Principles, the Effects Database, or Standard Solutions by applying each tool within each box.
