# Chapter 20: Innovation Process Management

## Core Idea
Innovation doesn't happen by accident — it requires a structured process. The TRIZ-based Innovation Process Management framework (from Livotov's TRIZ Handbook) provides a 10-step methodology that integrates TRIZ tools into a complete innovation pipeline: from strategic analysis through idea generation, evaluation, concept development, implementation, and post-launch controlling. It's designed to make innovation repeatable, measurable, and systematic rather than dependent on occasional flashes of genius.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **10-Step Innovation Process (Livotov)**: A full innovation pipeline in two phases — 5 strategic steps + 5 execution steps.
  - When to use: Setting up an R&D/innovation department; designing a new product development process; institutionalizing innovation in an organization; managing a portfolio of innovation projects.
  - When NOT to use: One-off problem solving (use the 5-step TRIZ process from Ch11 instead). For a single inventor working alone.

- **Innovation Controlling System**: KPIs, stage-gate reviews, and feedback mechanisms for managing the innovation pipeline.
  - When to use: When innovation efforts feel unfocused; when you can't tell which projects are working; when management demands innovation ROI metrics.

## Key Concepts

- **Strategic vs Execution Phases**: Strategy (Steps 1-5) defines WHERE and WHY to innovate. Execution (Steps 6-10) defines WHAT and HOW. Most organizations skip strategy and jump to execution — this is the #1 cause of innovation failure.
- **Innovation Bottleneck**: The specific point in the value chain where improvement would create the most leverage. TRIZ tools (Function Analysis, Trimming, Ideality) identify this systematically rather than by gut feel.
- **Innovation Goals**: Specific, measurable target parameters derived from strategic diagnosis. "Improve customer retention" is a wish; "Reduce churn rate from 5% to 2% through 3 new retention features" is an innovation goal.
- **Stage-Gate with TRIZ**: At each gate, TRIZ tools evaluate not just business case but technical feasibility, ideality progression, and contradiction resolution status.
- **Innovation Portfolio Management**: Using S-Curve analysis to balance projects across infancy (exploratory), growth (scaling), and maturity (optimization) stages.

## The 10-Step Process

```
PHASE I: STRATEGIC INNOVATION MANAGEMENT

Step 1: SITUATION ANALYSIS
  Tools: Market analysis, technology landscape, competitive intelligence, patent analysis
  Output: Current state map — where we are vs competitors vs technology frontier
  Key question: "What's happening in our market and technology space?"

Step 2: STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS
  Tools: Function Analysis of business model, Value Stream Mapping, Bottleneck Analysis
  Output: Identified innovation bottlenecks — the highest-leverage points for improvement
  Key question: "Where is innovation most needed and most impactful?"

Step 3: INNOVATION GOAL SETTING
  Tools: IFR formulation for the business, Ideality KPIs, target parameter definition
  Output: Specific, measurable innovation targets with timelines
  Key question: "What exactly do we need to achieve by when?"

Step 4: STRATEGY DEVELOPMENT
  Tools: Contradiction Matrix (business parameters), Trends of Evolution, S-Curve positioning
  Output: Innovation roadmap — which TRIZ tools to apply to which goals
  Key question: "How will we achieve our innovation goals?"

Step 5: STRATEGIC CONTROLLING
  Tools: Balanced Scorecard adapted for innovation, KPI dashboards, feedback mechanisms
  Output: Measurement system + feedback loops
  Key question: "How will we know if our innovation strategy is working?"

═══════════════════════════════════════════════

PHASE II: OPERATIONAL INNOVATION EXECUTION

Step 6: IDEA GENERATION
  Tools: 40 Principles, Separation Principles, Effects Database, Trends, AFI (inverted for opportunities)
  Output: Large pool of solution concepts (aim for 50-100+ raw ideas per goal)
  Key question: "What are ALL the possible ways to achieve this innovation goal?"

Step 7: IDEA EVALUATION
  Tools: PUGH Matrix, Ideality assessment, multi-criteria decision analysis, feasibility screening
  Output: Ranked shortlist of best concepts (top 5-10)
  Key question: "Which ideas have the highest ideality and feasibility?"

Step 8: CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT
  Tools: ARIZ for hard sub-problems, Su-Field for interactions, Trimming for simplification
  Output: Detailed solution designs, specifications, initial prototypes
  Key question: "How do we turn the best ideas into working solutions?"

Step 9: IMPLEMENTATION
  Tools: Implementation methodology (see Ch22), prototyping, testing, IP protection
  Output: Validated, patent-protected, production-ready solutions
  Key question: "How do we bring the concept to market?"

Step 10: INNOVATION CONTROLLING
  Tools: Post-launch KPI tracking, ideality trend monitoring, feedback loops → back to Step 1
  Output: Performance data, lessons learned, continuous improvement triggers
  Key question: "Did we achieve the innovation goals? What's next?"
```

## Mental Models

- **Innovation is a process, not an event.** Organizations that treat innovation as brainstorming sessions get random ideas. Organizations that treat it as a managed process get systematic, cumulative advantage.
- **Strategy before execution, always.** The 5 strategic steps are not optional warm-up. They prevent the most expensive innovation mistake: building the wrong thing brilliantly.
- **The output of innovation management is decisions, not ideas.** The process doesn't end at idea generation (Step 6) — it ends at implemented, validated, and controlled solutions (Step 10). An unimplemented idea has zero value.
- **Feedback closes the loop.** Step 10 feeds back into Step 1 for the next cycle. Innovation management is continuous, not a one-time project.

## Anti-patterns

- **Skipping strategic diagnosis (Step 2)**: Jumping straight to "we need more ideas" without understanding WHERE innovation is needed produces scattered, low-impact ideas.
- **Idea generation without evaluation criteria**: Brainstorming without clear evaluation criteria (Step 3's targets) produces ideas that feel good but don't move the needle.
- **Treating the process as waterfall**: In practice, steps overlap and iterate. A concept development issue (Step 8) may send you back to idea generation (Step 6). The process is structured but not rigid.
- **Measuring innovation by number of ideas generated**: Ideas are input, not output. Measure implemented solutions, customer impact, and ideality improvement.

## Key Takeaways

1. Innovation management requires BOTH strategic direction (Steps 1-5) AND operational execution (Steps 6-10)
2. Strategic diagnosis identifies WHERE to innovate — skip this and you'll innovate in the wrong places
3. Define specific, measurable innovation goals BEFORE generating ideas — goals constrain and focus creativity
4. Generate 50-100+ raw ideas per goal before evaluating — quantity enables quality through selection
5. Evaluate ideas against ideality (benefits / costs+harms) and strategic fit, not just feasibility
6. Innovation controlling (Steps 5 and 10) provides the feedback that makes innovation continuously improve
7. The 10-step process is cyclical, not linear — Step 10 feeds back to Step 1
