# TRIZ Glossary

All significant TRIZ terms with precise definitions and chapter references. Based on *TRIZ For Dummies* (Haines-Gadd, 2016) and Livotov's *TRIZ Handbook*.

---

## A

**AFI (Anticipatory Failure Identification)** — A TRIZ tool for predicting and preventing failures before they happen. Inverts the problem: instead of "how to prevent failure," ask "how to cause catastrophic failure" using TRIZ tools, then re-invert to find design fixes. 4-step process: Invert → Exaggerate → Generate with TRIZ → Re-invert & Fix (Ch18).

**ARIZ (Algorithm of Inventive Problem Solving)** — The most advanced TRIZ tool; a step-by-step algorithm for solving highly complex, non-standard problems where no contradiction is initially apparent. Classical ARIZ-85 has 9 Parts and 26 Steps. Gadd's simplified 5-Step ARIZ: (1) Define Mini-Problem, (2) Identify Contradiction, (3) Apply IFR, (4) Apply Separation Principles, (5) Apply Standards & Effects (Ch3, Ch11, Ch17).

**Asymmetry** — Inventive Principle 4: change the shape of an object from symmetrical to asymmetrical; if already asymmetrical, increase the degree of asymmetry (Ch3, Appendix A).

---

## B

**Benefit Gap** — The difference between Importance (I) and Satisfaction (S) for a customer benefit dimension: Gap = I − S. Large positive gaps indicate high-priority innovation targets — the "Innovation Sweet Spot" (Ch21).

**Benefits** — The useful functions or positive outcomes a system delivers. Part of the Ideality equation numerator. TRIZ seeks to increase benefits while reducing costs and harms (Ch5).

**Business Contradiction** — Same structure as a Technical Contradiction but with business parameters: improving business metric A worsens business metric B. Resolved using the 12 Double Business TRIZ Principles (Ch19).

**Business Ideality** — Σ Customer Value / (Σ Costs + Σ Harms). The TRIZ Ideality equation applied to business models and organizational systems (Ch19).

**Business TRIZ** — Adaptation of classical TRIZ contradiction-resolution to business, management, and organizational problems. Uses 12 Double Principles, 5 Thinking Components, and business-specific resource analysis (Ch19).

---

## C

**CAI (Computer-Aided Innovation)** — Software tools that integrate TRIZ methodology to automate mechanical aspects: Effects Database search, semantic contradiction matching, patent analysis, Function-Oriented Search, and technology forecasting. CAI amplifies a TRIZ practitioner — it does not replace TRIZ understanding (Ch23).

**Concept Validation** — Stage 1 of the Implementation Pipeline: building a minimum proof-of-principle test to verify the core mechanism works before investing in detailed design. Goal: kill bad ideas cheaply (Ch22).

**Contradiction** — The core TRIZ concept: a situation where trying to improve one aspect of a system worsens another. Two types: Technical Contradiction (parameter A vs parameter B) and Physical Contradiction (one parameter must be in two opposite states simultaneously) (Ch3).

**Contradiction Matrix** — A 39x39 lookup table mapping improving parameters (rows) against worsening parameters (columns). Each cell recommends the top 4-5 Inventive Principles most frequently used in patents to resolve that specific contradiction (Ch3, Appendix B, Appendix C).

**Costs** — All expenditures required to deliver a system's benefits: materials, energy, space, time, information. Part of the Ideality equation denominator (Ch5).

**Customer Benefit Profile** — A structured decomposition of customer experience into discrete benefit dimensions, each rated for importance and current satisfaction. Used to identify Innovation Sweet Spots (Ch21).

---

## D

**Double Principles (12 Business TRIZ Principles)** — Each classical Inventive Principle is presented with its valid business opposite, because business problems often require balancing contradictory strategies. Examples: Segmentation ↔ Consolidation, Taking Out ↔ Integration, Universality ↔ Specialization (Ch19).

**Dynamism** — Inventive Principle 15: make an object or its environment adjustable to provide the best performance under different operating conditions. Key principle for resolving physical contradictions (Ch3, Appendix A).

---

## E

**Effects Database** — A TRIZ knowledge base cataloguing scientific effects (physical, chemical, geometric) that can deliver specific functions. Instead of reinventing mechanisms, you look up known effects that convert input X to output Y. Modern implementations are CAI searchable databases (Ch6, Ch23).

---

## F

**5 Thinking Components (Business TRIZ)** — A simplified mental framework: (1) Ideality (maximize value, minimize cost/harm), (2) Contradictions (identify and resolve business trade-offs), (3) Resources (find untapped business resources), (4) Patterns (recognize business evolution trends), (5) Effects (find business mechanisms that deliver desired outcomes) (Ch19).

**Function Analysis** — Systematic decomposition of a system into components, their interactions, and the functions they perform. Identifies harmful, insufficient, and excessive functions to reveal where innovation is needed (Ch12).

**Function-Oriented Search (FOS)** — A CAI technique that searches across industries for how a specific FUNCTION has been achieved, rather than searching for a specific component name. CAI's killer app — finds solutions from domains the engineer would never think to search directly (Ch23).

---

## H

**Harms** — Negative outcomes, side effects, or environmental damage caused by a system. Part of the Ideality equation denominator. TRIZ aims to eliminate or redirect harms (Ch5).

---

## I

**Ideal Final Result (IFR)** — A thought experiment: define the perfect solution where the system delivers all required functions with zero cost and zero harm. In ARIZ, IFR is formulated with extreme precision specifying operational zone, operative time, and required properties of the X-Element (Ch9, Ch17).

**Ideality** — The TRIZ value equation: Ideality = Σ Benefits / (Σ Costs + Σ Harms). All TRIZ tools aim to increase Ideality. A system tends toward ideal when it delivers maximum benefit with minimum cost and harm (Ch5).

**Implementation Pipeline (5 Stages)** — Concept Validation → Detailed Design → Prototyping & Testing → Production Ramp-Up → Post-Launch Monitoring. A structured approach to bridging the gap between TRIZ concepts and production-ready implementations (Ch22).

**Importance-Satisfaction Matrix** — A 2x2 grid plotting customer needs by Importance vs Satisfaction, revealing four zones: Innovation Sweet Spot (high I, low S — invest here), Competitive Parity (high I, high S — maintain), Overshoot (low I, high S — trim), Irrelevance (low I, low S — ignore) (Ch21).

**Innovation Controlling System** — KPIs, stage-gate reviews, and feedback mechanisms for managing the innovation pipeline. Steps 5 and 10 of the 10-Step Innovation Process provide measurement and feedback loops (Ch20).

**Innovation Sweet Spot** — High Importance + Low Satisfaction on the I-S Matrix. The highest-ROI innovation targets where TRIZ problem-solving effort should be focused (Ch21).

**Innovation Time Allocation** — The policy of reserving a percentage of engineering time for TRIZ/innovation work. Without protected time, TRIZ becomes "something we do when the firefighting stops" — which is never (Ch24).

**Inventive Principles (40)** — The 40 universal solution patterns distilled from millions of patents. Each principle is a conceptual trigger ("Segmentation", "Taking Out", "Merging", etc.) that has been used repeatedly across all engineering domains to resolve contradictions (Ch3, Appendix A).

---

## L

**Levels of Invention (1-5)** — Altshuller's classification of inventions by degree of innovation: Level 1 (routine design, 32% of patents), Level 2 (minor improvement, 45%), Level 3 (major improvement within a discipline, 18%), Level 4 (new paradigm using cross-discipline knowledge, 4%), Level 5 (pioneering discovery, <1%) (Ch1).

---

## M

**MATRIZ Certification (5 Levels)** — The international TRIZ competency standard: Level 1 (Foundation — 40 hrs), Level 2 (Practitioner — 80+ hrs + projects), Level 3 (Advanced Practitioner — 120+ hrs + supervised projects + exam), Level 4 (Expert — multi-year + methodology contributions), Level 5 (Master — selected by existing Masters, <100 worldwide) (Ch24).

**Mini-Problem** — The simplest possible formulation of a problem, stripped of all constraints, jargon, and "we can't because..." assumptions. ARIZ Step 1 forces this reduction as the foundation for all subsequent analysis (Ch17).

---

## N

**9-Windows (System Operator)** — A thinking tool that explores a problem across three time dimensions (past, present, future) x three scale dimensions (sub-system, system, super-system). Prevents tunnel vision and reveals hidden resources and constraints (Ch8).

**NIH (Not-Invented-Here) Syndrome** — Organizational resistance to solutions originating outside the team. TRIZ's systematic approach can trigger NIH because solutions appear to come from a method rather than from the team's own expertise (Ch22).

**Nested Doll (Matryoshka)** — Inventive Principle 7: place one object inside another; make one part pass through a cavity in another. Named after Russian nesting dolls (Ch3).

---

## O

**Operative Time (OT)** — The exact time window when the conflict occurs, separated from pre-conflict and post-conflict time. Defined in ARIZ Part 2 (Resource Analysis) to focus the solution search temporally (Ch17).

**Overshoot Zone** — High Satisfaction + Low Importance on the I-S Matrix. You're over-investing — resources should be trimmed and redirected to Innovation Sweet Spots (Ch21).

---

## P

**Patent Circumvention** — CAI-based systematic analysis of competitor patents using TRIZ Trimming and Substitution to find legal design-arounds (Ch23).

**Physical Contradiction** — One parameter of a system must simultaneously be in two opposite states (hot and cold, big and small, rigid and flexible). Resolved using Separation Principles: separate in Time, Space, Condition, or System (Ch3, Appendix D).

**Prototype Escalation** — A deliberate 5-level sequence from low-fidelity to high-fidelity: (1) Paper/thought experiment, (2) Simulation/CAE, (3) Breadboard/3D-printed functional prototype, (4) Engineering prototype, (5) Production prototype. Each level reduces risk before committing to the next (Ch22).

**Psychological Inertia** — Mental blocks that prevent seeing novel solutions: functional fixedness, expert blindness, anchoring to current design, and assumptions about constraints. TRIZ provides specific tools (SLP, Size-Time-Cost, 9-Windows, IFR) to overcome each type (Ch7).

---

## R

**Resistance Management** — Systematic approach to overcoming organizational, technical, and psychological resistance to new solutions. Treats resistance as information (often pointing to real constraints) rather than obstruction (Ch22).

**Resources** — Everything available in and around the system that can be used to improve ideality: substance resources (materials, waste), field resources (energy, gravity, magnetism), space resources (voids, unused volume), time resources (idle periods, parallel operations), information resources (data, signals), and functional resources (exploiting existing functions for new purposes) (Ch5).

---

## S

**S-Curve** — The lifecycle model of technical systems: infancy (slow growth), rapid growth, maturity (plateau), decline. Understanding where a system sits on the S-Curve determines whether to optimize, innovate radically, or move to a new S-Curve. Applies equally to business models and markets (Ch4, Ch19).

**Secondary Problem Cascade** — Mapping all sub-problems that a TRIZ solution creates. Solving one contradiction often creates 5-10 smaller problems that must be resolved for implementation. Each secondary problem is itself a mini-TRIZ problem (Ch22).

**Semantic TRIZ Search** — NLP-based automatic matching of natural language problem descriptions to TRIZ knowledge bases (Effects Database, 40 Principles, Standard Solutions) without requiring manual parameter classification (Ch23).

**Separation Principles** — Four strategies for resolving Physical Contradictions: separate conflicting requirements in Time (at different times), Space (in different locations), Condition (under different conditions), or System (transition to super-system or sub-system) (Ch3, Appendix D).

**Size-Time-Cost** — A creativity tool that stretches thinking by mentally varying an object's size (0 -> infinity), operating time (0 -> infinity), and cost (0 -> infinity). Breakthrough ideas often emerge at the extremes (Ch7).

**Smart Little People (SLP)** — A mental modeling tool where you imagine the system as populated by intelligent little people who can move, hold, push, and decide. Used to understand conflicts at the micro-level and discover resolution mechanisms (Ch7). Integrated into ARIZ Part 4 for resource mobilization (Ch17).

**Stage-Gate** — Decision points in the innovation process where projects are evaluated against criteria. With TRIZ, Stage-Gate evaluates not just business case but also technical feasibility, ideality progression, and contradiction resolution status (Ch20).

**Standard Solutions (76)** — A classified system of 76 solution patterns organized into 5 classes based on Substance-Field (Su-Field) analysis. Classes: (1) Build/destroy Su-Fields, (2) Evolve Su-Fields, (3) Transition to super-system/micro-level, (4) Detect and measure, (5) Apply standards to standards. More structured than the 40 Principles and applicable when the problem can be modeled as a Su-Field (Ch13, Appendix E).

**Substance-Field (Su-Field) Analysis** — A TRIZ modeling method representing any technical system as a triad: Substance 1 (S1), Substance 2 (S2), and a Field (F) acting between them. Problems are classified by what's wrong with the Su-Field triangle (missing element, harmful interaction, insufficient interaction) (Ch13).

---

## T

**Technical Contradiction** — The classic TRIZ contradiction: improving parameter A of a system causes parameter B to worsen. Resolved by finding an Inventive Principle that satisfies both. Example: increasing engine power (A) increases fuel consumption (B) (Ch3).

**10-Step Innovation Process (Livotov)** — A full innovation pipeline in two phases: Strategic (Steps 1-5: Situation Analysis -> Strategic Diagnosis -> Goal Setting -> Strategy Development -> Strategic Controlling) + Execution (Steps 6-10: Idea Generation -> Evaluation -> Concept Development -> Implementation -> Innovation Controlling). Cyclical — Step 10 feeds back to Step 1 (Ch20).

**Trends of Technical Evolution (8)** — Eight empirically derived patterns that predict how technical systems evolve over time: (1) Stages of Evolution (S-Curve), (2) Increasing Ideality, (3) Non-Uniform Development of Sub-systems, (4) Increasing Dynamism and Controllability, (5) Increasing Complexity then Simplification, (6) Matching and Mismatching of Parts, (7) Transition to Micro-level, (8) Increasing Automation. Used for product roadmapping, patent strategy, and predicting competitor moves (Ch4).

**Trimming** — Systematic elimination of system components while preserving or transferring their useful functions to remaining components. The most powerful cost-reduction tool in TRIZ. Ask: "Is this component necessary? Can another component perform its function? Can the function be eliminated entirely?" (Ch14). Applied to business as organizational trimming — eliminating departments/processes while preserving function (Ch19).

**TRIZ (Teoriya Resheniya Izobretatelskikh Zadach)** — Theory of Inventive Problem Solving. Created by Genrich Altshuller (1926-1998) through analysis of over 2 million patents to identify universal patterns of innovation. TRIZ proves that innovation follows predictable rules, not random inspiration (Ch1).

**TRIZ Deployment Maturity Model** — Organizational maturity ladder: Level 0 (Unaware) -> 1 (Awareness — isolated projects) -> 2 (Adoption — formal training) -> 3 (Integration — TRIZ embedded in process) -> 4 (Culture — default approach) -> 5 (Excellence — contributes to TRIZ methodology) (Ch24).

**TRIZ Tourist** — A person who knows TRIZ terminology from a short course but cannot apply it to real problems. The Training Duration Rule states minimum 5 days (40 hours) for Level 1 Foundation; compressing below this threshold produces TRIZ tourists (Ch24).

---

## X

**X-Element** — An unknown substance or field that resolves a contradiction. In ARIZ, you specify exactly what properties the X-Element must have (functional specification) before searching for what it actually is — preventing premature fixation on known technologies (Ch17).

---

## Z

**Zone of Conflict (ZoF)** — The exact spatial location where a contradiction physically manifests, distinguished from the broader operational zone. ARIZ zooms into this micro-location because solutions that change the whole system are usually over-designed — conflict resolution lives at the zone of conflict (Ch17).
