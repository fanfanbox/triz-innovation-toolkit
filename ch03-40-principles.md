# Chapter 3: Solving Contradictions with the 40 Inventive Principles

## Core Idea
The hardest problems are contradictions -- situations where improving one thing makes something else worse, or where you want opposites of the same thing. TRIZ identified exactly 40 fundamental ways to resolve contradictions, distilled from analyzing over 50,000 inventive patents.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Technical Contradiction**: when improving one parameter makes another parameter worse.
  - When to use: a proposed solution creates a downside; someone says "that won't work because..."; you make a change and something else breaks.
  - How: (1) identify what gets BETTER and what gets WORSE, (2) map both to the 39 Engineering Parameters (see Appendix C of the book), (3) look up their intersection in the Contradiction Matrix (Appendix B), (4) apply the suggested Inventive Principles (Appendix A).

- **Physical Contradiction**: when you want opposite properties from the SAME thing.
  - When to use: you need something to be both big AND small, hot AND cold, rigid AND flexible, sharp AND blunt.
  - How: (1) state the contradiction in plain language using "I want X but also want not-X", (2) determine how you can SEPARATE the opposites (see below), (3) use the Separation Principles table to find which Inventive Principles apply.

- **Four Separation Strategies** (for Physical Contradictions):
  1. **Separate in Time**: opposites at DIFFERENT times (umbrella: big when raining, small when carrying)
  2. **Separate in Space**: opposites at the SAME time but DIFFERENT places (sticking plaster: sticky on skin, not sticky on wound)
  3. **Separate on Condition**: opposites at the same time and place but for DIFFERENT features (colander: catches pasta, lets water through; window: lets light in, keeps wind out)
  4. **Separate by System**: opposites at DIFFERENT levels of the system (bicycle chain: rigid to transmit force, flexible to go around gears; treadmill: you want to move without moving)

## Key Concepts
- **Contradiction**: an unresolved conflict in what you want -- either between two parameters (Technical) or within one parameter (Physical). Contradictions only exist because of the CURRENT way you are achieving your outcome.
- **Contradiction Matrix**: a 39x39 lookup table built from statistical analysis of patents. For each pair of improving/worsening parameters, it lists the Inventive Principles most frequently used to resolve that specific contradiction.
- **39 Engineering Parameters**: the generic categories into which any technical trade-off can be mapped (e.g., #1 Weight of Moving Object, #9 Speed, #14 Strength, #27 Reliability, #39 Productivity). These strip away domain-specific language.
- **40 Inventive Principles**: the complete set of conceptual solution triggers (e.g., Segmentation, Taking Out, Local Quality, Dynamism, Parameter Change, Colour Change). Full descriptions with examples are in Appendix A of the book.
- **Separation Principles**: a mapping showing which of the 40 Inventive Principles are most useful for each of the four separation strategies (Time, Space, Condition, System).
- **Five Levels of Invention**: patents ranked by inventiveness -- Level 1 (32%, within narrow specialty), Level 2 (45%, within industry), Level 3 (18%, from other industries), Level 4 (4%, from science), Level 5 (<1%, beyond contemporary science). The Inventive Principles were drawn ONLY from Levels 3-5.

## Mental Models
- **Turn every "that won't work because..." into a Technical Contradiction to solve.** When a colleague objects to your idea, they are giving you the two parameters for the Contradiction Matrix. Thank them and look it up.
- **Use the Contradiction Matrix as a treasure map.** Brainstorming is digging at random; the Matrix tells you which four or five spots have yielded the most gold for this exact type of contradiction.
- **Use the Principle you want to reject.** If an Inventive Principle seems completely inapplicable (e.g., Colour Change for a mechanical problem), lean into it -- it leads to solutions nobody in your field would intuitively consider.
- **Use Physical Contradictions for non-technical problems.** Unlike Technical Contradictions, you can express Physical Contradictions in plain language without mapping to parameters -- ideal for business, management, and process problems.

## Anti-patterns
- **Compromising instead of resolving**: picking a middle ground (e.g., medium-sized refrigerator) gives you neither the big capacity you want nor the small footprint. TRIZ demands both -- find a different solution approach.
- **Solving the wrong problem**: if you skip the contradiction definition step and jump straight to generating ideas, you may cleverly solve a problem that does not actually move you toward your ideal outcome.
- **Rejecting invented Principles that seem "too abstract"**: the Principles are deliberately conceptual. "Parameter Change" can mean changing flexibility, temperature, concentration, consistency, density, or phase. Each sub-meaning is a separate trigger.
- **Treating the Matrix as an absolute rule**: the Contradiction Matrix records what worked MOST OFTEN in the past. If a suggested Principle genuinely does not apply, try the next one. Also consider defining your contradiction with different parameter pairs.

## Worked Example
**The cheese grater problem**: you want to grate cheese very fast, but a bigger grater scatters cheese everywhere (a Technical Contradiction: faster grating vs. loss of control).

Step 1 -- Map to parameters:
- What is getting better? Faster grating -> Parameter 39: Productivity
- What is getting worse? Lost cheese over a larger area -> Parameter 6: Area of Stationary Object

Step 2 -- Look up the intersection in the Contradiction Matrix. Productivity (39) vs. Area of Stationary Object (6) yields Principles: 10, 35, 17, 7.

Step 3 -- Apply each Principle:
- **10. Prior Action**: buy pre-grated cheese, or spread newspaper underneath
- **35. Parameter Change**: make the grater flexible so it is big for grating but curves to direct the cheese
- **17. Another Dimension**: curve the grater and lengthen it -- a 3D arc gives large surface area while guiding cheese (shown in the book as the Todo giant grater by Ricard Sapper for Alessi)
- **7. Nested Doll**: place a container inside/beneath the grater to catch cheese (shown as the Parmenide grater by Alejandro Ruiz for Alessi)

Each Principle was applied by combining the conceptual trigger with domain knowledge about kitchen tools.

## Key Takeaways
1. Contradictions feel impossible only because you assume the conflicting properties are inherently linked. They are not -- that link exists only in the current solution approach.
2. Always define the contradiction before generating solutions. As Einstein said: "If I had one hour to save the planet, I would spend 59 minutes defining the problem."
3. The Contradiction Matrix does not mention cheese, engines, or software -- you must first abstract your problem into the 39 Engineering Parameters.
4. Technical Contradictions are easier to spot when working with others (they will tell you what is wrong with your idea). Physical Contradictions are easier to describe (plain language, no parameter mapping needed).
5. Every Technical Contradiction contains a Physical Contradiction inside it -- translating between them can unlock new solution paths.
6. The 40 Principles are conceptual triggers, not ready-made answers. They require your domain knowledge and creativity to turn into practical solutions.
7. The Separation Principles table tells you which of the 40 Inventive Principles to apply based on how you can separate your contradiction (Time, Space, Condition, or System).
